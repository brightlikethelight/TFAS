\begin{abstract}
Steering along a single linear direction in activation space shifts
LLM cognitive-bias susceptibility by 37.5 percentage points, but only
in standard models.  In reasoning models, the same direction is
\emph{readable but not writable}: probes decode it, yet causal
interventions along it fail.  This dissociation is our central finding.
We compare matched-architecture pairs---Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct vs.\
R1-Distill-Llama-8B, OLMo 7B/32B Instruct vs.\ Think---and
Qwen-3-8B's thinking toggle, across 470 contrastive items spanning 11
bias categories.  Reasoning training collapses heuristic lure rates:
27.3\% to 2.4\% (Llama), 19.6\% to 0.4\% at 32B.  Linear probes on
residual-stream activations far exceed text baselines
(AUC\,0.974/0.930 vs.\ 0.840), confirming the signal lives in
geometry, not surface tokens.  Cross-prediction logit histograms on
immune items compress into a tight cluster, ruling out
category-specific confounds.  Probe-direction steering
produces a 37.5pp dose-response shift in Llama (lure$\to$correct,
zero garbage outputs), while R1---steered continuously across all 2048 tokens
at four layers including its probe peak---shows no coherent
dose-response at any layer, ruling out both temporal washout
and a deeper writable locus.  Within-chain-of-thought probing reveals a
non-monotonic trajectory (T0\,=\,0.973, T75\,=\,0.754,
Tend\,=\,0.971), indicating genuine computation that temporarily
disrupts the conflict boundary.  Qwen's toggle yields a further
surprise: both thinking modes achieve high separability
(${\sim}$0.97), but cross-mode probe transfer is at chance
(AUC\,0.496), indicating the two modes' conflict-encoding axes do not
align.  Scaling to 32B amplifies
standard-model vulnerability (14.9\%$\to$19.6\%) while reasoning
training remains effective (0.4\%).  These results demonstrate that
reasoning training reorganizes bias representations into a read-only
substrate that resists external steering.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Kahneman's dual-process framework distinguishes fast, heuristic
processing (``System~1'') from slow, deliberative processing
(``System~2'') \citep{kahneman2011thinking}.  While the binary framing
is an acknowledged oversimplification---modern accounts treat this as a
\emph{continuum} of processing intensity \citep{melnikoff2018mythical,
evans2013dual, deneys2023advancing}---the core observation that
cognitive systems flexibly allocate effort, with measurable consequences
for bias susceptibility, remains robust.  De~Neys' conflict
detection paradigm \citep{deneys2012bias, deneys2017dual} demonstrates
that humans often \emph{detect} conflicts between heuristic and
normative responses even when they fail to override the heuristic---a
dissociation between conflict \emph{monitoring} and conflict
\emph{resolution} with clear neural correlates in the anterior cingulate
cortex \citep{botvinick2001conflict}.

LLMs exhibit analogous patterns: instruction-tuned models reproduce
human-like errors on conflict items while succeeding on matched controls
\citep{hagendorff2023human}, and reasoning-trained models resist many
such lures \citep{deepseekr1}, though the protection is bias-specific
rather than universal \citep{schwartz2025clinical}.  A growing literature
documents these parallels \citep{xie2025cogsci, codaforno2025dual,
ziabari2025reasoning}, and \citet{huang2026cogbias} showed that
bias-relevant directions are probeable and steerable.  We ask a
different question: what does reasoning post-training change about the
\emph{decodability}, layer location, and \emph{behavioral writability}
of those directions?

Yet behavior alone cannot distinguish genuine processing-mode differences
from surface mimicry---chain-of-thought traces are often unfaithful,
with only ${\sim}2.3$\% of reasoning steps causally influencing the
final answer \citep{jiang2025aha, lanham2023measuring,
boppana2026reasoning}.  The critical question is whether fast/slow modes
have \emph{mechanistic correlates} in internal representations, and how
those correlates change when a model is trained to reason.

Activation steering \citep{turner2023activation, rimsky2024steering}
shows that adding steering vectors to residual stream activations can
modulate behavior along interpretable dimensions.  This raises a natural
question: if linear probes identify a direction separating \sone{}-like
from \stwo{}-like processing, can steering along it \emph{causally}
modulate bias susceptibility---moving findings from correlational to
mechanistic?

We address this question with a natural experiment: Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct
and DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B share identical architectures (32
layers, 32 attention heads, 4096 hidden dimensions) but differ in
reasoning distillation training.  Representational differences are
thus primarily attributable to the post-training pipeline, not
architecture.  We make six contributions:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item A \textbf{contrastive cognitive bias benchmark} of 470 items
  (235 matched conflict/control pairs) across 11 categories spanning
  four heuristic families (representativeness, cognitive reflection,
  decision framing, and loss/availability), with novel isomorphs to resist
  memorization.

  \item \textbf{Behavioral validation} showing a dramatic gap: 27.3\%
  overall lure rate (standard) vs.\ 2.4\% (reasoning), replicated on
  OLMo-3-7B (14.9\% $\to$ 0.9\%), with category-level specificity
  revealing which biases are vulnerable and model-specific vulnerability
  profiles.

  \item \textbf{Probing analysis} revealing that the standard model has
  high internal conflict/control separability (AUC\,=\,0.974) while the
  reasoning model renders this distinction \emph{less linearly accessible} (AUC\,=\,0.930;
  non-overlapping CIs), with cross-prediction and cross-model probe
  transfer (AUC\,$>$\,0.92) confirming specificity to a shared
  processing-mode direction.

  \item \textbf{Causal evidence via probe-direction steering}: steering along the probe's weight vector reduces lure rates on vulnerable categories, establishing a \emph{causal} link between the probe direction and bias susceptibility.

  \item \textbf{Within-CoT probing}: probe AUC follows a non-monotonic trajectory---peaking at onset (0.973), dropping during deliberation (0.754), rebounding before the answer (0.971)---ruling out decorative reasoning.

  \item \textbf{A training--inference dissociation}: Qwen~3-8B's think/no-think modes achieve similar conflict separability (AUC\,=\,0.954 and 0.970) but cross-mode probe transfer is at chance (AUC\,=\,0.496)---the two modes develop non-transferring conflict-encoding axes.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent We also analyze \textbf{scale effects} at 32B
(OLMo-3.1), finding that scale \emph{increases} standard-model
vulnerability while reasoning training remains protective, and report
converging SAE and attention entropy evidence.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Dual-process cognition in LLMs.}
\citet{hagendorff2023human} first documented human-like cognitive biases
in LLMs, finding that instruction-tuned models reproduce
representativeness and anchoring effects that vanish in later ChatGPT
versions.  \citet{codaforno2025dual} applied psychometric methods to show
that heuristic and deliberative prompts elicit shared early-layer
representations but diverge in later layers---a suggestive but purely
behavioral result.  \citet{ziabari2025reasoning} positioned reasoning
along a continuous spectrum rather than a binary, examining how reasoning
training shifts the balance between fast and slow processing modes.
\citet{brady2025dual} provide a comprehensive review in \emph{Nature
Reviews Psychology}, arguing that dual-process theory offers a productive
(if imperfect) lens for AI cognition research.
Our work moves beyond behavioral characterization: we test whether the
\sone{}/\stwo{} distinction has a \emph{mechanistic} counterpart in
model internals.

\paragraph{Cognitive bias benchmarks and probing.}
\citet{malberg2025cognitive} surveyed 30 cognitive biases across 20 LLMs,
establishing that bias susceptibility varies systematically with model
scale and training recipe, but without examining internal representations.
Most directly relevant, \citet{huang2026cogbias} demonstrated that
cognitive bias directions are linearly decodable and steerable in LLM
residual streams, achieving 26--32\% bias reduction via contrastive
activation addition.  We differ from CogBias in three respects:
(i)~architecturally matched base--reasoning model pairs that isolate
reasoning training as the independent variable, (ii)~Hewitt--Liang
control tasks and cross-domain transfer tests as probe validity checks
\citep{hewitt2019control}, and (iii)~causal evidence via probe-direction
steering on a contrastive benchmark with matched conflict/no-conflict
items.

\paragraph{Activation steering and representation engineering.}
Activation addition \citep{turner2023activation} and its variants
\citep{rimsky2024steering, li2024inference} demonstrate that linear
steering vectors can modulate model behavior at inference time.
CogBias \citep{huang2026cogbias} applied this to cognitive bias (26--32\%
reduction), but used mean-difference vectors.  Our probe-direction
steering uses the probe's optimized weight vector, providing a tighter
causal link.  For SAE analysis, we adopt Ma et al.'s falsification
protocol \citep{ma2026falsification} as a mandatory filter, since
45--90\% of purported ``reasoning features'' are spurious token-level
artifacts \citep{meloux2025dead}.

\paragraph{Conflict detection in cognitive science.}
Our framework draws on De~Neys' conflict detection paradigm
\citep{deneys2012bias, deneys2017dual}: humans detect heuristic--normative
conflicts even when they fail to override the heuristic.
\citet{evans2013dual} refined the Type~1/Type~2 taxonomy as graded rather
than binary.  \citet{botvinick2001conflict}'s conflict monitoring theory
provides the computational account.  We test the LLM analogue: do models
show internal conflict-detection signatures even when producing
heuristic-driven responses?

\section{Benchmark}
\label{sec:benchmark}

We construct a contrastive cognitive bias benchmark following three
design principles: (1) every conflict item has a structurally matched
no-conflict control \citep{deneys2012bias}; (2) all items use novel
surface features to resist memorization; and (3) category diversity
enables specificity analysis.

The benchmark comprises \textbf{470 items} organized as \textbf{235
matched pairs} across 11 categories spanning four heuristic families:
\emph{representativeness} (base rate neglect, 35 pairs; conjunction
fallacy, 20; belief-bias syllogisms, 25),
\emph{cognitive reflection} (CRT variants, 30; arithmetic, 25),
\emph{decision framing} (framing effects, 20; anchoring, 20), and
\emph{loss/availability} (sunk cost, loss aversion, certainty effect,
availability; 60 pairs total).  Base rate items include 10 pairs in
Gigerenzer's natural frequency format \citep{gigerenzer1995improve}.
Each item is scored as \emph{correct}, \emph{S1-lure} (the specific
heuristic-predicted wrong answer), \emph{other-wrong}, or \emph{refusal}.
Classic problems (e.g., the original bat-and-ball) serve only as
contamination baselines.

\paragraph{Item counts and analysis subsets.}
Our benchmark contains 470 items across 11 categories.  Primary probe
analyses use the 3 vulnerable categories (160 items).  Cross-prediction
uses all 7 original categories (350 items).  The full 11-category set
includes 4 additional categories added to test domain generality.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Models}

Our headline comparison exploits the architectural identity between
\textbf{Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct} \citep{llama31} and
\textbf{R1-Distill-Llama-8B} \citep{deepseekr1}: both have 32
transformer layers, 32 query heads (8 KV heads via grouped-query
attention), and 4096 hidden dimensions.  The models differ primarily in that
R1-Distill-Llama-8B was distilled from a reasoning-trained teacher.  We
additionally evaluate \textbf{Qwen~3-8B} in both thinking (\texttt{/think})
and non-thinking (\texttt{/no\_think}) modes, providing a
\emph{within-model} toggle of reasoning behavior on identical weights.
For cross-architecture replication, we evaluate
\textbf{OLMo-3-7B-Instruct} and \textbf{OLMo-3-7B-Think}
\citep{olmo3}, which share the same OLMo base but differ in reasoning
training.  For scale analysis, we evaluate
\textbf{OLMo-3.1-32B-Instruct} and \textbf{OLMo-3.1-32B-Think}
(64 layers, 4.5$\times$ the parameters of the 7B variant), enabling
a within-family scale$\times$reasoning-training comparison on identical
benchmark items.

\paragraph{Prompting and decoding.}
All models use greedy decoding (\texttt{do\_sample=False}, \texttt{temperature=1.0})
with no system prompt. For Qwen~3-8B, we use the hard mode switch
(\texttt{enable\_thinking=True/False} in \texttt{apply\_chat\_template}).
In no-think mode, this appends an empty
\texttt{<think>{\textbackslash}n{\textbackslash}n</think>{\textbackslash}n{\textbackslash}n}
block after the assistant turn marker, so the token sequences
\emph{diverge} after that marker: think-mode \pzero{} is the
newline after \texttt{assistant}, while no-think-mode \pzero{} follows
the injected \texttt{</think>} close tag.  Cross-mode probe
comparisons therefore test whether conflict encoding generalizes
across these distinct positional contexts. This is distinct from
Qwen's soft switch (\texttt{/think}, \texttt{/no\_think} tokens). For R1-Distill, the model naturally generates
\texttt{<think>...\allowbreak</think>} blocks without prompting;
we parse these to extract thinking-trace positions.
We use \texttt{max\_new\_tokens=128} for standard models and
\texttt{max\_new\_tokens=2048} for reasoning models to accommodate
chain-of-thought traces. We acknowledge this departs from vendor-recommended
settings (e.g., DeepSeek recommends \texttt{temperature=0.5--0.7});
Appendix~\ref{app:robustness} reports multi-seed robustness analysis with sampled decoding
(\texttt{temperature=0.7}) showing qualitatively consistent results.

\subsection{Activation Extraction}

For each model--item pair, we extract residual stream activations
(post-MLP, post-residual) at all layers at position \pzero{}: the
last prompt token before generation.  This ``pre-decision'' position
captures the internal state from which the answer is generated.
Activations are stored in BFloat16 (HDF5).  For within-CoT probing
(Section~\ref{sec:within_cot}), we additionally extract activations
at 10 evenly-spaced token positions within the reasoning trace.

\subsection{Linear Probes with Hewitt--Liang Controls}
\label{sec:probes}

We train $\ell_2$-regularized logistic regression probes
(\texttt{LogisticRegressionCV}, 5-fold stratified cross-validation) on
residual stream activations at each layer.  The probe target is binary:
\emph{conflict} vs.\ \emph{no-conflict} condition, restricted to the three
vulnerable categories (base rate neglect, conjunction fallacy, syllogisms)
where the standard model shows non-trivial lure rates.

\paragraph{Controls.}  We apply Hewitt--Liang control tasks
\citep{hewitt2019control}: probes trained on randomly permuted labels
establish a selectivity floor.  Selectivity (true AUC minus control AUC)
below 5 percentage points indicates the signal reflects probe capacity,
not representational structure.  We report ROC-AUC as the primary metric
with bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals (1000 resamples).

\paragraph{Immune categories.}  We separately probe the four categories
where models are largely immune (lure rates $\leq$10\% in most models;
CRT, arithmetic, framing, anchoring) as a \emph{negative control}: if
probes achieve high AUC on categories where there is minimal behavioral
difference, the signal is confounded by surface features rather than
processing mode.

\subsection{Probe-Direction Steering}
\label{sec:methods_steering}

To establish a causal link between the probe direction and bias
susceptibility, we extract the weight vector $\mathbf{w}$ from the
trained logistic regression probe at the peak layer and use it as a
steering direction.  At inference time, we add $\alpha \cdot \hat{\mathbf{w}}$
(where $\hat{\mathbf{w}} = \mathbf{w} / \|\mathbf{w}\|$) to the
residual stream at the intervention layer, sweeping $\alpha$ over a
range of positive (toward \stwo{}) and negative (toward \sone{}) values.
We evaluate the steered model on the 80 vulnerable-category conflict
items and report lure rate changes as a function of steering strength
$\alpha$; specificity controls on matched non-conflict items appear in
Appendix~\ref{app:steering_specificity}.

Four findings emerge.  First, the behavioral gap is dramatic: Llama produces heuristic lures at 27.3\%; R1-Distill reduces this to 2.4\%.  Second, vulnerability is \emph{category-specific}: base rate neglect, conjunction fallacy, and syllogisms are susceptible, while CRT, arithmetic, framing, and anchoring are largely immune.  Third, different models show distinct vulnerability profiles (Qwen: 95\% conjunction but 0\% syllogism lure), suggesting model-specific rather than universal biases.  Fourth, Qwen's within-model thinking toggle reduces lure rate from 21\% to 7.3\%.

The model \emph{detects} conflict pre-generation even when it subsequently fails to resolve it.

Llama peaks at AUC\,=\,0.974 [0.952, 0.992] at L16;
R1-Distill peaks at 0.930 [0.894, 0.960] at L31---non-overlapping CIs.  The 15-layer shift (L16\,$\to$\,L31 of 32) is architecturally suggestive.  Llama resolves conflict mid-network, leaving later layers to map the resolution to output tokens.  R1-Distill pushes the conflict representation to the penultimate layer, where the primary downstream operation is to set the hidden state for the chain-of-thought rollout \citep{zhang2025probing}.
Qwen~3-8B achieves high separability in both modes (no-think: 0.954; think: 0.970; both at L34), but cross-mode probe transfer is at chance (AUC\,=\,0.496, cosine $= -0.005$): probes trained in one mode do not generalize to the other.  Because the two modes' \pzero{} tokens occupy different sequence positions (Section~\ref{sec:methods}), this non-transferability reflects both the positional context shift and any mode-specific encoding reorganization.  The finding that within-mode separability is high (${\sim}$0.97) while cross-mode transfer fails shows that each mode develops its own conflict-encoding axis.

\paragraph{Selectivity and text-only baseline.}  Hewitt--Liang control probes achieve
AUC\,$\leq$\,0.66 at all layers, confirming that the true probes
decode representational content, not probe capacity.  Selectivity
exceeds 34 percentage points at peak layers for both models.\footnote{We
  train probes in two settings: 5-fold CV on all 350 items (7 categories,
  peak L14, AUC\,=\,0.999), used for cross-prediction and steering; and
  bootstrap CI analysis on 160 vulnerable-category items (3 categories,
  peak L16, AUC\,=\,0.974 [0.952, 0.992]).}
A global sentence-transformer text-only baseline (trained on all
categories, encoding only input text with no access to model
activations) achieves AUC\,=\,0.840, substantially below the
activation probe (0.999).  When evaluated out-of-distribution on
held-out syllogism items, the text baseline collapses to 0.086
(below chance, because deceptive surface similarity between conflict
and control syllogisms causes the text encoder to systematically
confuse the classes), while the activation probe remains at 0.999.
This confirms the activation probe captures processing-relevant
information unavailable from input text alone.

\paragraph{Token-length confound check.}
Prompt token lengths differ between conflict and control items in two
of three vulnerable categories (base rate: Cohen's $d\!=\!+3.69$;
conjunction: $d\!=\!+6.36$) but \emph{not} in syllogism ($d\!=\!+0.02$).
Three observations rule out length as the primary probe signal.
(i) A probe trained on syllogism alone (where length is balanced)
achieves within-category AUC\,=\,0.936 and transfers to base rate
(0.950) and conjunction (0.990) probes, showing the shared direction
is not length.  (ii) Cross-prediction of a base rate probe to syllogism
achieves 0.594---nontrivial without any length signal.  (iii) The
text baseline, which sees length directly as a feature, caps at 0.840;
the 16pp activation gap must come from non-text information.
Full per-category length statistics appear in
Appendix~\ref{app:length}.

\paragraph{Cross-prediction and immune-category specificity.}
Training on \emph{vulnerable} categories and testing on \emph{immune} categories yields near-chance transfer. \paragraph{Cross-model probe transfer.}

\subsection{Causal Evidence: Probe-Direction Steering}
\label{sec:steering}

We extract the probe weight vector $\mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^{4096}$ from a logistic-regression probe trained on the three vulnerable categories at layer~14 (3-category CV AUC\,=\,0.960), normalize to $\hat{\mathbf{w}}$, and add $\alpha \cdot \hat{\mathbf{w}}$ to the residual stream at inference time, sweeping $\alpha \in \{-5, \ldots, +5\}$ across the 80 vulnerable-category conflict items.

Figure~\ref{fig:steering} shows a monotonic dose-response: at $\alpha = -5$ (toward \sone{}), lure rate rises to 68.8\%; at $\alpha = +5$ (toward \stwo{}), it drops to 31.2\%---a 37.5pp causal swing, comparable to \citet{huang2026cogbias}'s 26--32\% reduction.  The entire lure reduction converts to correct answers (zero increase in ``other'' responses), ruling out distributional disruption.  Random-direction controls ($5$ unit vectors per $\alpha$) show no systematic effect (52--58\% lure across all $\alpha$), confirming the probe direction is \emph{causally sufficient} to modulate bias susceptibility.

\paragraph{Two specificity controls.}
(i)~Probe steering on matched \emph{non-conflict} items produces zero lure responses at any $\alpha$, with only minor coherence loss at $|\alpha|\!=\!5$ (Appendix~\ref{app:steering_specificity}): the direction is specific to the conflict subspace.  (ii)~The CogBias-style mean-difference vector (cosine $= 0.314$ with the probe) is a stronger one-sided debiasing intervention (lure 52.5\%$\to$11.25\% at $\alpha\!=\!+5$) but is \emph{unidirectional}---negative $\alpha$ has no effect (stays 53.75\%).  The probe direction is weaker for debiasing but \emph{bidirectional} ($+$16.25pp worse at $\alpha\!=\!-5$), making it better evidence of a causal boundary.

\paragraph{R1-Distill steering contrast: layer sweep.}
We sweep four layers in R1-Distill (L14, L25, L28, L31) under continuous
steering across the full 2048-token generation, directly addressing the
objection that steering at L14 (Llama's peak) may miss a deeper writable
locus in R1 (whose probe peaks at L31).  Table~\ref{tab:r1_layer_sweep}
shows the result: \emph{no layer produces a coherent dose-response.}
Lure rate fluctuates between 6--14\% with no monotonic trend at any
layer.  At L31 (R1's probe peak, AUC\,=\,0.930), lure is 10.0\% at
$\alpha\!=\!-5$ and 11.25\% at $\alpha\!=\!+5$, compared to a baseline
of 11.25\%---a 1.25pp range, versus Llama's 37.5pp swing.  The model
remains fluent at all layers and all $\alpha$ (correct rate $\geq 86\%$,
``other'' rate $\leq 14\%$).  The direction that is causally active in
Llama is not stably controllable in R1-Distill at \emph{any} layer, even
under continuous intervention: \emph{readable but not writable.}

\subsection{Within-CoT Probing}
\label{sec:within_cot}

We probe residual stream activations at seven canonical positions
for Qwen~3-8B (think mode) at L34: P0 (pre-generation), T0 (thinking
onset), T25/T50/T75 (quartile positions within the trace), Tend (final
thinking token), and P2 (control position).

\paragraph{Non-monotonic trajectory.}
Probe AUC follows a non-monotonic trajectory:
P0 (0.938) $\to$ T0 (0.973) $\to$ T25 (0.791) $\to$ T50 (0.772)
$\to$ T75 (0.754) $\to$ Tend (0.971); P2 returns exactly 0.500 (chance).
Three phases emerge: (i) \emph{crystallization} (P0$\to$T0): thinking onset sharpens the boundary by +3.5pp before any substantive reasoning tokens, consistent with ``fast initial commitment'' \citep{deneys2017dual}; (ii) \emph{exploration} (T0$\to$T75): AUC drops ${\sim}22$pp as mid-trace representations explore alternative framings; (iii) \emph{resolution} (T75$\to$Tend): AUC rebounds to 0.971, re-crystallizing the boundary.

This trajectory discriminates three hypotheses.  \emph{Performative reasoning} (deciding at T0, emitting decorative tokens) predicts flat AUC---rejected by the 22pp dip.  \emph{Monotonic sharpening} predicts steadily increasing AUC---rejected by the T0$>$T50 inversion.  Only \emph{exploration-before-exploitation} \citep{boppana2026reasoning} is consistent.  The trajectory also resolves how Qwen's thinking mode improves behavior despite orthogonal P0 encoding: the exploration and resolution phases perform genuine computational work that the no-think mode never executes.
Full position$\times$layer results appear in Appendix~\ref{app:within_cot}.

\subsection{Scale Analysis: 7B vs.\ 32B}
\label{sec:scale}

We evaluate OLMo-3.1-32B-Instruct and OLMo-3.1-32B-Think
(64 layers, 5120 hidden dimensions; 4.5$\times$ the parameters of the
7B variants) on the full 470-item benchmark to test whether scale
mitigates cognitive bias vulnerability.

\paragraph{Behavioral results.}
Scale \emph{increases} standard-model vulnerability: overall lure rate rises from 14.9\% (7B) to 19.6\% (32B), with base rate neglect nearly doubling (46\%$\to$74.3\%) and framing emerging as an entirely new vulnerability (0\%$\to$30\%).  Meanwhile, some biases are resolved at scale (loss aversion: 33\%$\to$0\%), and conjunction fallacy is scale-invariant (50\% at both sizes).
At 32B, reasoning training drops lure rates from 19.6\% to 0.4\%.  The OLMo family exhibits an \emph{asymmetric scale effect}: 4.5$\times$ more parameters increases standard-model vulnerability by +4.7pp while leaving reasoning-model performance essentially unchanged ($-$0.5pp).  This pattern---larger standard models reproducing \emph{more} human heuristic errors---mirrors inverse scaling phenomena \citep{mckenzie2023inverse, perez2023discovering} in which pre-training absorbs human biases and scale amplifies rather than corrects them.  Our contribution is to show that this inverse-scaling effect is \emph{fully cancelled} by reasoning post-training, even as absolute encoding fidelity of the bias direction itself continues to rise with scale.

\paragraph{Probe results at 32B.}
Probes on OLMo-3.1-32B-Instruct achieve AUC\,=\,0.9999 at L20/64; Think peaks at 0.9978 at the same layer.  The behavioral gap \emph{widens} with scale (14.0pp at 7B $\to$ 19.2pp at 32B), yet the representational gap \emph{shrinks} (0.034 $\to$ 0.0021)---both models encode conflict with near-identical fidelity at 32B, but only Think translates this into correct responses.  This reinforces the ``readable but not writable'' finding.  Full breakdowns appear in Appendix~\ref{app:scale}.

\subsection{SAE Features and Attention Entropy}
\label{sec:results_sae_attention}

SAE analysis of Llama's L19 residual stream identified 41 features with
significant conflict/control differential activation (BH-FDR $q < 0.05$),
all surviving Ma et al.\ falsification \citep{ma2026falsification}.  The

frame (e.g., F27821 triggers on ``professional'', ``random'', ``no''---
the ``no particular selection criterion'' base-rate-control vocabulary).
A third group (3 S2-graded features) activates more strongly on controls
without being binary.  Attention entropy analysis reveals R1-Distill has
nearly twice as many \stwo{}-specialized heads as Llama (57 vs.\ 30 of
1024; BH-FDR $q < 0.05$, $|r_{rb}| \geq 0.3$).  Details in
Appendix~\ref{app:sae_attention}.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Conflict detection without resolution.}
The standard model \emph{detects} conflict (AUC\,=\,0.974) yet fails
to resolve it (27.3\% lure rate), paralleling De~Neys' finding
\citep{deneys2012bias} that human conflict monitoring often occurs even
when the heuristic prevails.
The reasoning model shows \emph{less} distinct encoding (AUC\,=\,0.930)
yet dramatically better resolution (2.4\%), replicated in OLMo
(AUC 0.996 $\to$ 0.962; lure 14.9\% $\to$ 0.9\%).
Reasoning distillation makes Type~2 processing \emph{autonomous}
\citep{evans2019reflections}---internalized so thoroughly that a sharp
conflict signal is no longer needed \citep{venhoff2025base}.

\paragraph{Readable but not writable.}
In Llama, the probe direction is readable (AUC\,=\,0.974) and
writable (37.5pp bidirectional causal swing); in R1-Distill, it is
readable (AUC\,=\,0.930) but not detectably writable at \emph{any}
layer (L14, L25, L28, L31) under continuous intervention across the
full 2048-token reasoning trace.  The layer sweep rules out the
hypothesis that the writable locus simply moved deeper with reasoning
training---direct evidence for the S2-by-default hypothesis
\citep{evans2019reflections}.
Qwen's think/no-think modes both achieve high separability
(${\sim}$0.97), but cross-mode transfer is at chance (0.496),
partly reflecting different \pzero{} positional contexts
(Section~\ref{sec:methods}).
At 32B, standard-model lure rises while reasoning training remains
protective (0.4\%).  Seven controls against artifacts appear in
Appendix~\ref{app:robustness}.

\section{Within-CoT Probing Details}
\label{app:within_cot}

This appendix details the within-chain-of-thought probing protocol
for R1-Distill-Llama-8B and Qwen~3-8B (think mode).  The analysis
tests whether reasoning traces perform genuine computational work
on conflict representations or are merely decorative post-hoc
rationalizations---a critical question given that only ${\sim}2.3$\%
of reasoning steps causally influence final answers
\citep{jiang2025aha, lanham2023measuring}.

\subsection{Method}

For each reasoning model--item pair, we extract residual stream
activations at five canonical positions within the
\texttt{<think>...\allowbreak</think>} trace:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{T0}: First token of the thinking trace (immediately
  after \texttt{<think>}).  This captures the ``initial impression''
  before any explicit reasoning.
  \item \textbf{T25}: Token at 25\% of the trace length.
  \item \textbf{T50}: Midpoint of the trace.
  \item \textbf{T75}: Token at 75\% of the trace length.
  \item \textbf{Tend}: Final token before \texttt{</think>}.
  This captures the representation after reasoning is complete
  but before the answer is generated.
\end{itemize}

\noindent At each position, we extract the full residual stream
(post-MLP, post-residual) at all layers.  We then train independent
$\ell_2$-regularized logistic regression probes (5-fold stratified CV)
at each (layer, position) combination on the conflict vs.\ no-conflict
classification, restricted to the three vulnerable categories (base
rate neglect, conjunction fallacy, syllogisms; $n$ = 160).

\paragraph{Non-monotonic trajectory.}
The data decisively support Hypothesis~3 (non-monotonic trajectory):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item AUC \emph{increases} from P0 (0.938) to T0 (0.973): the
  onset of thinking immediately sharpens the conflict representation.
  \item AUC then \emph{drops} by ${\sim}20$pp across T25--T75
  (0.791$\to$0.754): deliberation temporarily disrupts the linear
  separability of conflict and control representations.
  \item AUC \emph{rebounds} to 0.971 at Tend: the final thinking
  token recovers near-peak separability before the answer is generated.
  \item P2 returns exactly 0.500 (chance), confirming that the probe
  captures position-specific content rather than global signal.
\end{itemize}

\noindent This pattern is consistent with ``exploration before
exploitation'' \citep{boppana2026reasoning}: intermediate reasoning
steps explore alternative framings or consider conflicting evidence,
temporarily destabilizing the conflict/control boundary, before
converging on a resolution that re-crystallizes the distinction.
The T0$\to$Tend near-identity (0.973 vs.\ 0.971) combined with the
mid-trace dip rules out both performative reasoning (which predicts
flat AUC) and simple monotonic sharpening (which predicts a steady
increase).

\subsection{Connection to the Qwen Dissociation}

The within-CoT results resolve the Qwen dissociation reported in
Section~\ref{sec:results_probes}.  At \pzero{}, Qwen~3-8B's two modes
achieve high conflict separability (AUC\,=\,0.954 no-think; 0.970 think
at L34), but their probe directions are orthogonal (cross-mode
AUC\,=\,0.496, cosine $= -0.005$).  Yet thinking reduces lure rates
from 21\% to 7.3\%.  The within-CoT data show that the thinking
trace itself performs genuine representational work: the T25--T75
dip (0.754--0.791) demonstrates active computation on the conflict
representation, and the Tend rebound (0.971) shows this computation
resolves successfully.

The behavioral improvement from Qwen's thinking mode is thus
mediated by in-context representational change during the trace,
not by training-induced encoding differences.  The orthogonal P0
directions mean the two modes start from different representational
vantage points, and the thinking trace navigates from one to a
resolution.  This confirms that training and inference-time
thinking are mechanistically distinct: training reorganizes the
\sone{}/\stwo{} encoding (Llama vs.\ R1-Distill), while
inference-time thinking operates \emph{within} an orthogonal
encoding to dynamically resolve conflicts during generation.

\paragraph{Discussion: why scale increases vulnerability.}
The 32B model achieves near-perfect probe separability
(AUC\,=\,0.9999 at L20) yet shows \emph{higher} behavioral
vulnerability (19.6\% vs.\ 14.9\%).  This dissociation---better
conflict detection with worse conflict resolution---parallels the
Llama vs.\ R1-Distill pattern at 8B scale and mirrors De~Neys'
finding in humans that conflict monitoring does not guarantee override
\citep{deneys2012bias}.

We propose two complementary explanations for why scale increases
vulnerability:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Richer heuristic representations.}  Larger models
  encode more diverse world knowledge, which may strengthen
  representativeness-based reasoning.  A 32B model trained on more
  data develops stronger priors about category membership (e.g., the
  ``stereotypical lawyer/engineer'' association in base rate items),
  making it harder to override these priors with statistical information.

  \item \textbf{Expanded surface sensitivity.}  The emergence of
  framing vulnerability at 32B (0\% $\to$ 30\%) suggests that scale
  enables sensitivity to subtle linguistic cues (gain vs.\ loss
  framing) that smaller models simply cannot detect.  In this view,
  scale grants the capacity to be influenced by framing effects that
  smaller models are too impoverished to represent.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent Both explanations are consistent with the
``conflict detection without resolution'' pattern: the 32B model
detects conflict (AUC\,=\,0.9999) but its larger representation space
also encodes stronger heuristic attractors that resist override.
The plateau in probe AUC from L20 onward (all layers $\geq$0.996)
confirms that scale does not alter the \emph{nature} of the
\sone{}/\stwo{} distinction---only its behavioral consequences.

whether a feature's activation is driven by token-level artifacts

\paragraph{Protocol.}  For each feature:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item Identify the top-3 tokens that maximally activate the feature
  across all benchmark items.
  \item Inject each token into 100 random non-cognitive-bias texts
  (drawn from a held-out corpus of Wikipedia paragraphs, news
  articles, and code snippets).
  \item Measure the feature's activation in the random context.
  \item A feature is declared \emph{spurious} if its mean activation
  in random contexts exceeds 50\% of its mean activation in the
  benchmark context.
\end{enumerate}

  to influence the outcome.

Probe results at \pzero{} are \textbf{unaffected} by
generation strategy, because \pzero{} activations are deterministic
given the prompt.  The pre-decision representation is identical
regardless of whether the model subsequently generates via greedy or
sampled decoding.  This confirms that our probing results (AUC
values, cross-prediction, steering) reflect stable properties of the
model's internal representations rather than artifacts of a particular
generation strategy.

The probe--behavior divergence under sampling further supports the
``conflict detection without resolution'' interpretation: the model
\emph{always} encodes the conflict in its \pzero{} representation
(AUC\,=\,0.930 regardless of decoding), but whether it successfully
\emph{resolves} that conflict depends on the stochastic reasoning
path it takes during generation.

\paragraph{Why length is not the primary signal.}
Three observations rule out length as the dominant confound.  First,
a probe trained on syllogism alone (where length is balanced,
$d\!=\!+0.02$) achieves within-category AUC\,=\,0.936 and transfers
to base rate (0.950) and conjunction (0.990)---the shared direction
this probe recovers cannot be length.  Second, length direction
is inconsistent across categories: conjunction/base rate have
$d\!>\!0$ while framing/certainty effect have $d\!<\!0$, so a
pure-length probe would produce maximally anti-correlated cross-category
predictions, not the ``immune-cluster compression'' pattern we
observe in the logit-histogram analysis (Section~\ref{sec:results_probes}).  Third, the sentence-transformer
text baseline, which has direct access to length as a feature,
caps at 0.840; the 16pp gap to the activation probe (0.999) is
information not extractable from the prompt text alone.

\paragraph{Specific OLMo-32B audit.}
OLMo-3.1-32B-Instruct achieves probe AUC\,=\,0.9999 on the vulnerable
three categories.  Since syllogism (length-balanced) is one of those
three, the probe cannot be purely length-driven: if it were, it
would collapse to chance on syllogism and drag the joint AUC below
0.67.  The high joint AUC implies the probe separates syllogism
conflict vs.\ control at high accuracy despite near-identical
prompt lengths (93.3 vs.\ 93.2 tokens).

\paragraph{Three observations.}
(i) The probe direction produces \emph{zero} lure responses on control
items at any $\alpha$: the direction is specific to the S1/S2 conflict
subspace and does not generically perturb answers.  The small drop in
correctness at $\alpha\!=\!-5$ on control items (78.75\%) reflects
residual-stream coherence loss (the 21.25\% of items become ``other''
responses, not lures), the same pattern seen with random directions
in the main text.  (ii) The probe-weight direction is \emph{bidirectional}:
moving toward S1 (negative $\alpha$) increases lure by 16.25pp,
moving toward S2 (positive $\alpha$) decreases lure by 21.25pp.
Bidirectional dose-response is stronger evidence of causal sufficiency
than a unidirectional effect.  (iii) The mean-difference baseline is
\emph{unidirectional}: negative $\alpha$ has essentially no effect
(lure stays around 53--55\%), while positive $\alpha$ strongly
reduces lure (to 11.25\% at $\alpha\!=\!+5$).  Mean-difference
steering efficiently improves behavior but cannot test causal
sufficiency by worsening it---a key methodological motivation for
probe-weight steering.
