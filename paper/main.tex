\begin{abstract}
Large language models exhibit both heuristic and deliberative processing
patterns on cognitive bias tasks: they fall for intuitive lures on conflict
problems while succeeding on structurally matched controls.  But do these
behavioral patterns reflect genuinely distinct internal computations, or
merely surface-level mimicry?  We present the first unified mechanistic
characterization of dual-process-like cognition in LLMs, combining five
complementary methods---linear probes, sparse autoencoder (SAE) feature
analysis with built-in falsification, attention entropy profiling,
representational geometry, and causal interventions---on a contrastive
cognitive bias benchmark of 284 items across 7 task categories.  Our
headline comparison exploits a natural experiment: Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct
and DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B share identical architectures but differ
in reasoning distillation training, isolating the effect of training on
internal representations.  Rather than testing for a binary System~1 versus
System~2 dichotomy, we measure a graded \emph{deliberation-intensity
gradient} across model layers and positions.  [We find that \ldots.
Placeholder for headline results: layer-wise probe accuracy, number of
genuine SAE deliberation features post-falsification, causal steering
effect sizes, and cross-model convergence patterns.]  Our results
[support/complicate/refute] the hypothesis that dual-process-like
computation has identifiable mechanistic substrates in LLMs, with
implications for understanding when models reason versus pattern-match.
\end{abstract}
