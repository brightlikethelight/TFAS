# Final ICML MechInterp Workshop Abstract

**Word count target: ≤250**

---

Does reasoning training change how LLMs internally represent cognitive biases, or merely suppress biased outputs? We investigate using matched-architecture model pairs that share identical weights and structure but differ in reasoning training: Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct vs. R1-Distill-Llama-8B, and OLMo-3-7B-Instruct vs. OLMo-3-7B-Think. Behaviorally, reasoning training dramatically reduces heuristic lure rates across architectures: 27.3% to 2.4% (Llama) and 14.9% to 0.9% (OLMo). We probe residual stream activations with linear classifiers to separate conflict from no-conflict items across 235 matched pairs spanning 11 cognitive bias categories. The standard Llama model achieves near-perfect conflict/control separability (peak AUC = 0.974, 95% CI [0.952, 0.992]), but the reasoning model shows *lower* separability (AUC = 0.930 [0.894, 0.960]; non-overlapping CIs)—the internal heuristic/deliberative boundary is blurred, not sharpened, by reasoning training. Cross-prediction confirms specificity: a probe trained on vulnerable categories yields transfer AUC = 0.378 on immune categories, capturing processing mode rather than surface features. Attention analysis reveals the reasoning model has nearly twice as many S2-specialized heads (57 vs. 30; BH-FDR corrected), suggesting redistribution of deliberative computation across the network. A within-model dissociation using Qwen-3-8B's thinking toggle sharpens the picture: inference-time thinking reduces lure rates (21% to 7%) without changing pre-generation probe signatures (both AUC = 0.971). Training changes S1/S2 encoding; inference-time thinking does not. Finally, natural frequency framing—which reduces base rate neglect in humans—paradoxically *increases* it in both models, demonstrating that ecological rationality does not transfer to LLMs.
